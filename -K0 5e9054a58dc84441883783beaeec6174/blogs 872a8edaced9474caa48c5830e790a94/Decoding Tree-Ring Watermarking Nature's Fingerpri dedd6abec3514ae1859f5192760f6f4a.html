Understanding the Foundations:

At the heart of this method lies the Fourier frequency, a concept that transforms our traditional understanding of images from mere color and brightness to a symphony of waves.



So what is Fourier frequency ?

let's break down the concept of Fourier frequency in a simple way, especially as it relates to images:

Imagine you have a beautiful picture made out of tiny little tiles (pixels). Each tile has a certain color that blends together to form the full picture. Now, suppose you wanted to understand the pattern of how these colors change across the picture—like where it goes from light to dark or how smoothly the colors change.

Fourier transform is like a magical tool that helps us see these patterns not in terms of colors and tiles, but in terms of waves. It tells us about different waves in the picture:

Slow waves (low frequencies): These are like the broad, gentle waves in the ocean that show the big, smooth changes in color in the picture. For example, a clear sky in a sunset photo that gradually changes from orange to purple.

Fast waves (high frequencies): These are like tiny, rapid ripples on a pond. They show where there are sharp, quick changes in color. For example, the sharp edge between a building and the sky in a cityscape.
When someone talks about Fourier frequencies in an image, they're referring to these waves and how quickly they rise and fall (change).

In watermarking, using this Fourier idea, you can sneak in some extra tiny waves (changes) that are so subtle they're invisible unless you know exactly how to look for them with special tools. This way, you can mark the image as yours without actually changing how it looks to us when we just enjoy the view.

What is Fourier Space?

Fourier space, often referred to as the frequency domain, is a conceptual framework used to analyze and represent the frequency components of signals or images. When we talk about Fourier space, we're essentially looking at how a signal (like sound) or an image can be broken down into its individual frequencies—think of these as the distinct tones or visual patterns that combine to form the overall sound or picture.

How It Works for Images:

  1. From Spatial to Frequency Domain: Normally, we see images in the spatial domain, where each point (pixel) has a position and a color or intensity. Fourier space transforms this view from position-based to frequency-based. It tells us about the presence of patterns, their orientations, and how they change across the image.
  1. Low and High Frequencies: In Fourier space, low frequencies represent the general, slow-changing parts of an image—like a smooth gradient of colors in a sunset. High frequencies represent the rapid changes—like the detailed edges of clouds or trees against the sky.

Visualizing Fourier Space:

If you were to visually represent Fourier space of an image, it might look like a plot or a graph with points scattered across it, where:

  • The center of the plot represents the lowest frequencies (less detail, more general features).
  • The edges of the plot represent the highest frequencies (more detail, sharp edges and textures).

Practical Use:

Fourier space is particularly useful in image processing for tasks such as:

  • Filtering: Removing noise (which often appears as high-frequency information) or blurring (which reduces high-frequency content) can be accomplished by manipulating the frequency components in Fourier space.
  • Compression: By understanding which frequencies contain the most important information, images can be compressed effectively by keeping the essential frequencies and discarding the less important ones.

Fourier Space in Tree-Ring Watermarking:

  1. Embedding the Watermark: The Tree-Ring Watermarking technique involves embedding a watermark directly into the initial noise vector of a diffusion model. Instead of altering the final image, the watermark is integrated into the Fourier space of this noise vector. Fourier space, representing frequency components, allows the watermark to be embedded in a way that it becomes an intrinsic part of the image generation process, rather than just an overlay or an afterthought.
  1. Pattern in Fourier Space: The specific pattern used for the watermark is structured in the Fourier space. This structuring ensures that the watermark is invariant to common image transformations like rotations, crops, and flips. These invariances are critical because they make the watermark robust against attempts to remove or alter it without significantly impacting the visual quality of the generated image.
  1. Detection Through Inversion: The detection of the watermark involves inverting the diffusion process to retrieve the original noise vector and then analyzing its Fourier space. By examining this space, the presence of the structured watermark can be confirmed. This method of detection is sophisticated because it requires reversing the model's image generation process to check for the watermark, making it secure against unauthorized detection.

Why Fourier Space is Ideal Here:

  • Invisibility: Watermarking in the Fourier space allows the watermark to remain invisible in the spatial (normal viewing) domain. Since Fourier space deals with frequencies, the modifications made for the watermark do not typically alter the visible aspects of the image directly but rather its underlying frequency components.
  • Robustness: By embedding the watermark in the frequency domain, it becomes inherently more resistant to image processing techniques that might otherwise degrade or remove a spatial-domain watermark. This includes resistance to resizing, compression, and various forms of noise interference.

does it mean that it cannot be changed or inversed ?

Fourier space is both changeable and invertible:

  • Changeable: You can modify Fourier space to alter the original signal or image in controlled ways. For instance, removing high-frequency components from Fourier space (a process known as low-pass filtering) can smooth an image or reduce noise.
  • Invertible: The process of transforming a function to Fourier space is reversible through the Inverse Fourier Transform. This means that after modifications in Fourier space, you can reconstruct the original signal or image, potentially with some alterations (like added watermarks or filtered noise).

How does Tree Ring achieve this ?

The Tree-Ring Watermarking technique described in the paper achieves robustness against common image transformations and does not require additional training or fine-tuning through a few key strategies in how it embeds and detects the watermark:

1. Embedding Watermark in the Initial Noise Vector:

The Tree-Ring Watermark embeds a pattern directly into the Fourier space of the initial noise vector used by the diffusion model for image generation. This approach has several advantages:

  • Integration with Sampling Process: Because the watermark is embedded at the very beginning of the image generation process, it becomes an intrinsic part of the image as it evolves through the model's layers. This deep integration helps maintain the watermark's presence even if the final image undergoes significant transformations.
  • Fourier Space Embedding: By placing the watermark in the Fourier space, the technique leverages properties that make it resistant to geometric transformations like rotation, flips, and crops. Fourier transforms have inherent qualities where rotation in the image space corresponds to a similar rotation in the Fourier space, and similarly for other transformations. This alignment ensures the watermark persists regardless of such changes.

2. Robustness Against Transformations:

The pattern used in the watermark (described as a Tree-Ring pattern in the Fourier space) is designed to be invariant to typical image manipulations such as:

  • Crops and Flips: Since the watermark is distributed across the Fourier space and not tied to specific spatial locations, cropping or flipping the image does not necessarily remove the watermark.
  • Color Jitter and Noise: The use of Fourier space for embedding the watermark also means that modifications like color adjustments or added noise, which primarily affect the image's spatial domain, are less likely to disrupt the frequency-based watermark.
  • Dilation and Rotation: The circular nature of the Tree-Ring pattern in the Fourier space means that rotations and dilations (which expand or contract the image) will similarly affect the watermark, allowing it to remain detectable.

3. Detection by Controlled Parties:

Detection of the watermark requires reversing the image generation process to recover the initial noise vector and inspecting its Fourier transform for the watermark pattern. This process can only be performed efficiently by those who have access to the specific model parameters and architecture used to generate the image. This exclusivity adds a layer of security, as unauthorized parties cannot easily detect or tamper with the watermark.

4. No Need for Additional Training:

Unlike some deep learning-based watermarking approaches, Tree-Ring Watermarking does not require retraining the model or fine-tuning it with watermarked examples. The watermarking process is a plug-in addition that operates on the model's input (the initial noise vector) rather than altering the model itself. This simplicity and lack of dependency on the training data streamline the implementation and deployment in various applications.

What will happen if i alter the image by rotation, scaling, flipping and cropping?

When an image is transformed into Fourier space (via a Fourier transform), it represents the image in terms of its frequency components rather than its spatial pixels. The key attributes of Fourier space relevant to transformations are:

  1. Rotation: A rotation in the image space corresponds to a similar rotation in Fourier space. This property ensures that if an image is rotated, its frequency representation rotates in the same way, but the essential characteristics of the frequencies (like the watermark) remain intact.
  1. Scaling and Flips: Similar to rotations, scaling (resizing) and flipping an image will alter the Fourier space representation accordingly, but the fundamental patterns (such as those used for watermarking) can be designed to remain detectable despite these changes.

What Happens When You Crop an Image:

  • Cropping and Fourier Space: Cropping an image reduces it to a part of the original image. In the spatial domain, this is simply cutting out a section; however, in the Fourier domain, the effect is not as straightforward. Cropping affects all frequency components because it changes the boundaries and contents of the image, thereby altering how frequencies are distributed across the image.
  • Impact on Watermark: If a watermark is embedded in Fourier space (especially using a technique like Tree-Ring Watermarking), it is typically dispersed throughout the frequency spectrum. Cropping the image will modify the frequency representation, but since the watermark is not localized to a specific area (but rather distributed), significant parts of the watermark can still be present in the cropped image’s Fourier transform.

Robustness to Cropping:

Here's how the robustness to cropping is maintained:

  • Distribution of Watermark: The watermark in the Fourier space is not concentrated in a small area but is spread across different frequencies. This distribution means that even after cropping, parts of the watermark will likely still exist within the Fourier spectrum of the cropped section.
  • Detection Post-Cropping: When detecting the watermark, the process involves converting whatever remains of the cropped image back to Fourier space and checking for the watermark's signature. Since the watermarking pattern (like the Tree-Ring structure) is designed to be recognizable even when partially present, it can still be detected.

Threat Model

The threat model defines the potential risks the watermarking system is designed to address. It considers three roles:

  1. Model Owner (Generation Phase): The owner of the diffusion model can generate images with a watermark that doesn't affect image quality. This watermark is meant to be a discrete signature verifying the image's origin.
  1. Forger: A forger might attempt to remove the watermark to use the image illicitly, claiming ownership or altering it for other purposes.
  1. Model Owner (Detection Phase): The owner must be able to detect the watermark in an image to prove its origin, even if the image has been altered, without knowing specific conditions under which the image was generated, like the text prompt or generation parameters.

Tree-Ring Watermarking Overview

This watermarking system works by inserting a specific pattern into the initial noise used by a diffusion model to generate images. This pattern, or "key," is placed in the Fourier space of the noise vector. Since diffusion models start with noise and iteratively refine it into an image, the watermark becomes part of the image naturally, without direct modification of the image itself.

The detection of the watermark requires reversing the image generation process to get back to the initial noise vector, then checking if the "key" is present. This key is inserted into the low-frequency part of the Fourier space, meaning it's subtly spread throughout the image and less susceptible to being removed by alterations like cropping or filtering.

Constructing the Tree-Ring Key

The watermark uses properties of the Fourier transform, which is a mathematical way to represent a signal (or image) by the frequencies that compose it. The "Tree-Ring" patterns are designed to be invariant to typical image manipulations:

  • A rotation in pixel space corresponds to a rotation in Fourier space.
  • A translation in pixel space multiplies all Fourier coefficients by a constant complex number.
  • A dilation/compression in pixel space corresponds to a compression/dilation in Fourier space.
  • Color jitter in pixel space (adding a constant to all pixels in a channel) corresponds to changing the magnitude of the zero-frequency Fourier mode..

Three key types are proposed:

  • Tree-RingZeros: This is the simplest, with zeros in a circular pattern in Fourier space. The key is chosen to be an array of zeros, which creates invariance to shifts, crops, and dilations. This key is invariant to manipulations, but at the cost of departing severely from the Gaussian distribution. It also prevents multiple keys from being used to distinguish between models.
  • Tree-RingRand: We draw the a fixed key k∗ from a Gaussian distribution. The key has the same id Gaussian nature as the original Fourier modes of the noise array, and so we anticipate this strategy will have the least impact on generation quality. This method also offers the flexibility for the model owner to possess multiple keys. However, it is not invariant to make image manipulations.
  • Tree-RingRings: This pattern comprised of multiple rings, and constant value along each ring. This makes the watermark invariant to rotations. The constant ring values from a Gaussian distribution. This provides some invariance to multiple types of image transforms, while also ensuring that the overall distribution is only minimally shifted from an isotropic Gaussian.

P-values and Watermark Detection

The watermark detection process involves statistical testing. For a given image, if the noise vector retrieved by reversing the diffusion process contains the watermark pattern, it's statistically unlikely to have occurred by chance. The authors propose a method to calculate a P-value, which quantifies this likelihood. If the P-value is below a certain threshold, it's safe to assume the watermark is present.

What is a P-value?

A P-value is a statistical measure that helps determine the significance of the results of an experiment or test. In this context, it's used to assess whether the watermark in an image is likely to have occurred by random chance.

The lower the P-value, the less likely it is that the watermark pattern could have occurred randomly, and therefore, the more confident we can be that the watermark was indeed present.
